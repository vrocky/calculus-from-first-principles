The Complaint

Nobody tells you where it comes from

Open almost any calculus course and the first thing you are handed is a chore: form the quotient $\dfrac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$, take a limit, call it the derivative. You can turn that crank for a year and never be told why — why the limit, why the factorials that show up later, why the Taylor series arrives like a magic trick in the final weeks of the course.

The derivative is taught as a procedure before it is ever shown as an idea. And the Taylor series — which is arguably the real object, the thing a function genuinely is near a point — is postponed to the very end, as if it were an advanced afterthought rather than the foundation.

This site takes the opposite road. We begin where the idea actually lives.

The Idea

Reality is layered. The layers are orders of smallness.

Picture the simplest hierarchy in geometry. A point has no length. A line is built from a length. A square is a length times a length. A cube, a length cubed.

Now shrink that length to something vanishingly small — call it $\Delta x$. Watch what happens to each shape:

The dimensional ladder

A tiny line has size $\Delta x$. A tiny square has size $\Delta x^2$ — and a square is infinitely smaller than the line, the way a line is infinitely smaller than the plane it sits in. A tiny cube, $\Delta x^3$, is smaller still.

So the infinitely small is not one thing. It comes in orders — a whole nested staircase of smallness, each step unreachably below the last. A first-order small. A second-order small. A third. A hidden world, stacked beneath the one we see.

A point is to a line what a line is to a square — and that single ratio, repeated, is the entire skeleton of calculus.

This is not a metaphor we will quietly drop. It is the engine. Everything that follows is just a careful reading of which layer each piece of a function lives on.

The Move

Disturb a function. Watch the ripple.

Here is the whole programme in one sentence. Take a function, nudge its input by an infinitesimal $\Delta x$, and the output does not respond at a single level — it ripples through every order of the hidden world at once:

$$f(x+\Delta x) \;=\; \underbrace{A_0}_{\text{order }0} \;+\; \underbrace{A_1\,\Delta x}_{\text{order }1} \;+\; \underbrace{A_2\,(\Delta x)^2}_{\text{order }2} \;+\; \underbrace{A_3\,(\Delta x)^3}_{\text{order }3} \;+\; \cdots$$

Each coefficient $A_n$ is the strength of the ripple on layer $n$. We do not yet know what these numbers are. But here is the beautiful part — we can fish them out one at a time, using nothing but algebra, because each layer is infinitely separated from the others. Divide by $\Delta x$ and the higher layers still vanish. Divide by $\Delta x^2$ and you reach into the square. The geometry guarantees the layers do not interfere.

When you carry this out — the peeling, layer by layer — the coefficients are forced to be exactly:

$$A_0 = f(x), \qquad A_1 = f'(x), \qquad A_2 = \frac{f''(x)}{2!}, \qquad A_n = \frac{f^{(n)}(x)}{n!}.$$

What just happened

The Taylor series was not assumed and it was not memorised. It fell out. It is simply the answer to the question, "how strongly does a disturbance ring on each layer of the hidden world?" The derivative is just the order-1 coefficient. The factorial is just the bookkeeping of counting. There is no magic — only a structure made visible.

Honesty

The world is real — and it is not the whole of reality

An honest foundation names its own edges. Two, in particular, are worth knowing from the start — and both make the picture deeper, not weaker.

There is no smallest number

Name any tiny positive number and half of it is smaller. So "the smallest number" does not literally exist. We mean something sharper: a quantity smaller than any you can name — and whose square is so much smaller still that, at the first layer, it is simply invisible. The hidden world is a world of relative smallness, not a smallest atom.

A basement below the layers

This whole method assumes a function's ripple actually reaches the layers — that it can be written as such a series at all. Almost every function you will meet obliges. But a few ghosts move without leaving a trace on any layer (the famous one is $e^{-1/x^2}$, whose every order reads zero, yet which is not zero). So the orders capture almost everything — and there is still a deeper basement beneath them. We start in the layered world; later, we learn the world goes further down than the layers.

Where this goes

One engine, the whole of calculus

Once you can read a function layer by layer, the rest of the subject stops being a list of rules to memorise and becomes a single idea, applied over and over:

The Keystone

Taylor by pure algebra

Peel the ripple layer by layer. The coefficients are forced. The factorial falls out of counting, not calculus.

 The Rules

Product & chain rule

Multiply two ripples, or feed one into another, and read off layer 1. The rules are patterns, not edicts.

 The Mirror

Integration & the FTC

Differentiation reads the first layer forward. Integration telescopes it backward. The same idea, reversed — which is why they are inverse.

That last point is the summit of the whole journey. In the usual course, the Fundamental Theorem — that slopes and areas are secretly inverse — lands like a miracle. Here it is structurally inevitable: it is the same coefficient, read in two directions.
