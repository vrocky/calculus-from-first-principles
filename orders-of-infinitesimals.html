Chapter One

The Hidden World

Before a single derivative, we need to see the staircase of smallness that everything else stands on.

Most people think the infinitely small is a single destination — a place called "zero" that quantities sink toward. It is not. The infinitely small is a structured world, with floors and basements, and the first job of calculus is to learn to read its address system.

We can build the whole thing from shapes a child already knows.

The construction

Point, line, square, cube

Take a length and shrink it until it is vanishingly small. Call that tiny length $\Delta x$. Now build the familiar shapes from it and ask how big each one is:

point
size 1
line
size Δx
square
size Δx²
cube
size Δx³

The line has length $\Delta x$. The square has area $\Delta x \times \Delta x = \Delta x^2$. The cube has volume $\Delta x^3$. Nothing surprising — yet. The surprise is in the ratios.

The key observation

How big is the tiny square compared to the tiny line? Their ratio is

$\dfrac{\Delta x^2}{\Delta x} = \Delta x \;\longrightarrow\; 0.$

The square is not just smaller than the line — it is infinitely smaller, in exactly the way a line is infinitely smaller than the plane it lies in. A line is a nothing of area. A point is a nothing of length. Each shape is an abyss below the one before it.

This gives us the staircase. Each shape lives on its own floor of smallness, and the floors are separated by unbridgeable gaps:

$$\underbrace{1}_{\text{point}} \;\ggg\; \underbrace{\Delta x}_{\text{line}} \;\ggg\; \underbrace{\Delta x^2}_{\text{square}} \;\ggg\; \underbrace{\Delta x^3}_{\text{cube}} \;\ggg\; \cdots$$

We call the floor a quantity lives on its order. The line is a first-order infinitesimal. The square is second-order. The cube, third-order. A point — a plain number that does not shrink at all — is order zero.

The infinitely small is not a number. It is a staircase, and every step is a private world.

Why it matters

The one property we will use everywhere

The staircase has a single feature that powers the entire subject, and it is worth stating on its own:

The separation principle

If you take the whole tower and divide it by $\Delta x$, every floor drops by exactly one step. The point becomes "infinitely large," the line becomes order zero, the square becomes order one, and so on. Divide by $\Delta x^2$ and everything drops by two.

This means we can aim at any single floor. Want to read what lives on the square? Divide by $\Delta x^2$ to lift the square down to order zero — where you can finally see it as an ordinary number — while everything above it is sent off to vanish and everything below stays buried. The orders never bleed into one another.

Hold onto that. It is the only tool we need. In the next chapter we will point it at a function and watch the Taylor series fall out — not as a theorem to be believed, but as the inevitable answer to a question about floors.

A first taste

The square already knows the product rule

Before we go further, here is a glimpse of how much the dimensional picture quietly contains. Grow the side of a square from $x$ to $x + \Delta x$ and ask how the area changes:

$$(x+\Delta x)^2 \;=\; \underbrace{x^2}_{\text{original square}} \;+\; \underbrace{2x\,\Delta x}_{\text{two strips}} \;+\; \underbrace{(\Delta x)^2}_{\text{corner square}}.$$

Look at the geometry literally. The new area is made of three pieces: the original square, two thin strips of size $x \times \Delta x$ along the edges, and one tiny corner square of size $\Delta x \times \Delta x$.

  • The two strips are first-order — length $x$, width $\Delta x$. Their total, $2x\,\Delta x$, is the real change.
  • The corner is second-order — a $\Delta x^2$ speck, a point-to-the-square. It is the part we are allowed to discard.

Throw away the corner, divide the strips by $\Delta x$, and you have just computed

$$\frac{d}{dx}\,x^2 = 2x$$

— not by a rule, but by seeing which floor each piece of the area lives on. The corner square is "negligible" for a reason you can point at: it is one whole order of smallness deeper than the strips. The dimensional intuition is not a picture of the derivative. It is the derivative.

The thread to follow

Every differentiation rule in this course is this same moment, dressed differently: write the change in orders of $\Delta x$, keep the first-order strips, discard the higher-order corners. Hold the image of the growing square. We will return to it again and again.
