The Full Arc

One Engine,
All of Calculus

Nine movements, a single idea — read a function's ripple layer by layer, forward to differentiate, backward to integrate.

Everything in this course runs on one machine, pointed in two directions. Forward: write $f(x+\Delta x)$ in orders of $\Delta x$ and read off the first floor — that is differentiation. Backward: write a difference of an antiderivative in orders of $\Delta x$ and let the sum telescope — that is integration. The two are the same act reversed, which is precisely why they undo each other.

The engine, stated once

To learn anything about a function near a point, expand it into the orders of the hidden world and look at the floor you care about. Differentiation rules read floor 1 of a combination. Integration reads floor 1 backward and lets the layers cancel. Nothing else is needed.

Movement II

The rules are patterns, not edicts

Take two functions, each with its own ripple, $f(x+h) = A_0 + A_1 h + \cdots$ and $g(x+h) = B_0 + B_1 h + \cdots$. Multiply them and collect by floor:

$$f(x+h)\,g(x+h) = A_0 B_0 + h\,(A_0 B_1 + A_1 B_0) + h^2(\cdots) + \cdots$$

Floor 1 reads $A_0 B_1 + A_1 B_0$ — which is $f(x)g'(x) + f'(x)g(x)$. That is the product rule, and it was nothing but the coefficient of $h$ when two ripples meet. Feed one ripple into another instead, and the coefficient of $h$ comes out $f'(g(x))\,g'(x)$ — the chain rule. The rules you once memorised are just what floor 1 says when functions combine.

Back to the growing square

The product rule is the two-strips-and-a-corner picture again. $A_0 B_1$ and $A_1 B_0$ are the two strips; the discarded $h^2$ term is the corner. Every rule in Part II is the growing square wearing a new costume.

Movements VI–VII · The Summit

Why slopes and areas are secretly the same

In the usual course, the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus arrives as a shock: why on earth should the slope of a curve and the area under one be inverse operations? They look nothing alike. Students take it on faith.

In this telling there is no shock, because both are the same coefficient read in opposite directions. Integration sums infinitesimal contributions; if $F$ is an antiderivative, each contribution is a floor-1 difference of $F$:

$$\int_0^N f(x)\,dx = \lim_{\Delta x\to 0}\sum_i \big[F(i\Delta x + \Delta x) - F(i\Delta x)\big].$$

Write the sum out and watch it telescope — every interior term cancels its neighbour, leaving only the ends:

$$\big[F(\Delta x) - F(0)\big] + \big[F(2\Delta x) - F(\Delta x)\big] + \cdots = F(N) - F(0).$$

$$\boxed{\int_0^N f(x)\,dx = F(N) - F(0).}$$

The structural punchline

Differentiation reads floor 1 forward. Integration reads floor 1 backward and lets the layers cancel. They are inverse not by coincidence but by construction — the same step, run the other way. The Fundamental Theorem stops being a miracle and becomes a sentence about a telescoping sum.

An honest seam

Where the world changes gear

One thing worth naming plainly. The first half of the journey (Parts I–V) lives in analytic-function land, where every function has a full ripple through the orders. The second half (Parts VI–IX) leans instead on finite differences and known summation patterns. Both are sound; they are two faces of the same idea — the algebra of smooth functions, and the algebra of accumulation. The bridge between them is the telescoping sum, where a difference on each small interval adds up to a difference at the ends. Knowing the gear-change is there is part of understanding the machine.

The lineage

Old roots, unfairly buried

This is not a novelty. Starting from series and reading coefficients is how the subject was born. Newton worked with infinite series as primary objects; Euler computed with them fearlessly; and the Kerala school of mathematics in India had power-series expansions for sine, cosine, and arctangent some two and a half centuries before Newton. The series-first view was the mainstream — until the nineteenth century replaced infinitesimals with limits to settle a crisis of rigour, and the intuitive layered world got branded "not rigorous" and pushed out of the introductory course.

By the twentieth century it had been vindicated — Abraham Robinson (1961) made infinitesimals fully rigorous again, and modern geometry formalised the "orders" as jets. The intuition you started with was right all along. The textbooks simply never got the memo.

A function is its series; the series is read one floor at a time; and the whole of calculus is that single act, forward and back.
