Chapter Two — The Keystone

Taylor, by Pure Algebra

No slopes. No areas. No memorised formulas. One assumption, and then we simply read the floors.

We now do the central thing this entire site is built on. We will not define the derivative and grind toward the Taylor series at the end of a long course. We will run it backward: assume a function ripples through the hidden world, and let the algebra force every coefficient into place. What emerges, with no further input, is the Taylor series — and the derivative as merely its first floor.

Step 0 — The one assumption

Postulate the ripple

Suppose that when we disturb the input of $f$ by an infinitesimal $\Delta x$, the output responds across every order of the hidden world:

$$f(x+\Delta x) = A_0 + A_1\,\Delta x + A_2\,(\Delta x)^2 + A_3\,(\Delta x)^3 + \cdots$$

The numbers $A_0, A_1, A_2, \dots$ are unknown — they measure how strongly the disturbance rings on floor $0$, floor $1$, floor $2$, and so on. We make no other assumption. We do not yet know what a derivative is. We will simply fish out each $A_n$ using the separation principle from the last chapter.

What we are really proving

This is an "if–then." If a function can be written this way at all, then its coefficients are forced to be one specific thing. That is a uniqueness theorem — and it is exactly how a working mathematician proves that a power series representation is unique. We are not asserting every function has a ripple; we are showing that when one does, its shape is not a matter of choice.

The peeling

Read the floors, one at a time

Floor 0 — the point

Set $\Delta x = 0$. Every term carrying a $\Delta x$ collapses, and only the ground floor survives:

$$f(x+0) = A_0 + A_1\cdot 0 + A_2\cdot 0^2 + \cdots = A_0.$$

Since $f(x+0) = f(x)$, the very first coefficient is simply the value of the function:

$$\boxed{A_0 = f(x).}$$

Floor 1 — the line

Subtract the ground floor we just found, then divide the whole tower by $\Delta x$. By the separation principle, every floor drops one step:

$$\frac{f(x+\Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x} = A_1 + A_2\,\Delta x + A_3\,(\Delta x)^2 + \cdots$$

Now let $\Delta x \to 0$. Everything still carrying a $\Delta x$ falls away into the basement, leaving exactly one number:

$$\lim_{\Delta x\to 0}\frac{f(x+\Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x} = A_1.$$

That left-hand side is what the rest of the world calls the derivative. In our telling, it was never the starting point — it is just the coefficient on the first floor:

$$\boxed{A_1 = f'(x).}$$

Floor 2 — the square

Peel off floors $0$ and $1$, then divide by $\Delta x^2$ to lift the square down to where we can see it:

$$\frac{f(x+\Delta x) - f(x) - f'(x)\,\Delta x}{(\Delta x)^2} = A_2 + A_3\,\Delta x + \cdots$$

Let $\Delta x \to 0$, and only $A_2$ remains. We will see in a moment — by counting, not by calculus — that this floor holds $\tfrac{1}{2}f''(x)$:

$$\boxed{A_2 = \frac{f''(x)}{2!}.}$$

Floor n — the pattern

Nothing about the procedure changes. To reach floor $n$, subtract everything below it and divide by $(\Delta x)^n$. One number survives, and it is always the $n$-th derivative divided by $n$-factorial:

$$\boxed{A_n = \frac{f^{(n)}(x)}{n!} \qquad n = 0,1,2,3,\dots}$$

Put the floors back together and there is the Taylor series — not imported, not believed, but assembled from the orders of the hidden world:

$$f(x+\Delta x) = f(x) + f'(x)\,\Delta x + \frac{f''(x)}{2!}(\Delta x)^2 + \frac{f^{(3)}(x)}{3!}(\Delta x)^3 + \cdots$$

Closing the one gap

Where the factorial really comes from

On floor 2 we claimed $A_2 = \tfrac{1}{2}f''(x)$ and kept moving. An honest foundation should not wave at that — so here is the factorial appearing out of pure counting, with no derivative borrowed from outside.

Sample the function at evenly spaced points $x, x+h, x+2h, \dots$ and feed each one into the ripple. Because $f(x+kh)$ carries $A_n$ with a factor $k^n$, the samples read:

$$\begin{aligned} f(x+h) &= A_0 + A_1 h + A_2 h^2 + A_3 h^3 + \cdots\\ f(x+2h) &= A_0 + 2A_1 h + 4A_2 h^2 + 8A_3 h^3 + \cdots\\ f(x+3h) &= A_0 + 3A_1 h + 9A_2 h^2 + 27A_3 h^3 + \cdots \end{aligned}$$

Now build a combination engineered to annihilate the lower floors. The "second difference" does exactly this:

$$f(x) - 2f(x+h) + f(x+2h) = 2\,A_2 + 6\,A_3\,h + 14\,A_4\,h^2 + \cdots$$

Floor 0 and floor 1 cancel perfectly — by design — and the leading survivor is $2A_2$. Divide by $h^2$ and take $h \to 0$:

$$A_2 = \frac{f(x) - 2f(x+h) + f(x+2h)}{2\,h^2}\bigg|_{h\to 0} = \frac{f''(x)}{2}.$$

The real origin of n!

Notice the $2$ in front of $A_2$, the $6$ that will sit in front of $A_3$, the $24$ in front of $A_4$. These are $2!, 3!, 4!$ — and they did not come from differentiating anything. They came from the binomial arithmetic of stacking the differences, from the $k^n$ that counting forces. In the standard course the factorial is a leftover from differentiating $\Delta x^n$ repeatedly. Here it is born from counting. Same number — humbler, more elementary origin.

What we have earned

The Taylor series was the foundation all along

Step back and see what the algebra has bought us with a single assumption and the separation principle:

  • The derivative is not a primitive idea. It is the coefficient on floor 1.
  • The Taylor series is not an advanced tool reserved for the end of the course. It is the most basic description of a function near a point — the full census of the hidden world.
  • The factorials are not mysterious. They are the cost of counting, the binomial weights that fall out when you stack samples.

The traditional ordering — limits, then derivatives, then, much later, series — turns out to be upside down. The series is the deepest object and the simplest to motivate. Everything else is a reading of one of its floors.

A function near a point is its ripple through the orders of the small. To differentiate is to ask which floor you want to hear.

From here, every other rule of calculus is the same move repeated. Multiply two ripples and read floor 1 — there is the product rule. Feed one ripple into another — there is the chain rule. Telescope the floors backward instead of forward — there is integration, and the Fundamental Theorem with it. The full journey lays them out, each one falling from this single idea.
